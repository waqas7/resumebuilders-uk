Applying for jobs in the UK? Format matters. British employers expect specific CV conventions that differ from US resumes. Here's what you need to know.

Getting the format right is not about making your CV look pretty — it is about meeting recruiter expectations, passing ATS screening, and presenting your experience in a way that British hiring managers recognise instantly. The wrong format signals unfamiliarity with the UK job market, even when your experience is strong.

## CV vs Resume in the UK

In the UK, "CV" (curriculum vitae) is the standard term for job applications at all career levels. It's typically 2 pages for experienced professionals and 1 page for entry-level roles.

Unlike US resumes, UK CVs traditionally include more personal detail — though modern trends are shifting toward privacy-conscious formats.

Key differences from US resumes:

| Feature | UK CV | US Resume |
|---|---|---|
| Name | CV or curriculum vitae | Resume |
| Length | 1–2 pages (standard jobs) | 1 page common |
| Photo | Not expected | Not expected |
| Personal details | City/region; no DOB | Minimal |
| Spelling | British English | American English |
| References | "Available on request" | Often omitted |

If you are applying to UK roles from abroad, convert your document to UK conventions before uploading. Recruiters notice American spelling, US date formats (MM/DD/YYYY), and resume terminology immediately.

## Essential Sections for a UK CV

1. **Personal Details** — Name, phone, email, city (full address optional)
2. **Personal Statement** — 3–4 line profile (similar to a professional summary)
3. **Work Experience** — Reverse chronological, with month/year dates
4. **Education** — GCSEs, A-Levels, degrees with grades if strong
5. **Skills** — Technical and soft skills relevant to the role

Optional sections depending on your background:

- **Professional Qualifications** — ACCA, CIPD, NMC, CSCS, etc.
- **Voluntary Experience** — Valuable for graduates and career changers
- **Projects** — Useful for tech, creative, and engineering roles
- **Languages** — Include proficiency level (native, fluent, conversational)

Do not include a "Hobbies and Interests" section unless your hobbies genuinely add value — marathon running for a fitness brand role, or coding side projects for a developer position. Generic entries like "reading and socialising" waste space.

## UK Formatting Conventions

- **Length:** 1–2 pages maximum
- **Photo:** Generally not required (and discouraged to avoid bias)
- **Date of birth:** No longer expected; omit for equality compliance
- **References:** "Available on request" is sufficient — don't list referees upfront
- **Spelling:** Use British English (organise, colour, programme)

Additional formatting details UK recruiters expect:

- **Date format:** Jan 2022 – Present (not 01/2022 or 2022/01)
- **Location:** City and region sufficient — "Manchester, Greater Manchester" not full street address
- **Job titles:** Use titles recognisable in the UK market — "Line Manager" not "Team Lead II" unless that is your actual title
- **File name:** Firstname-Lastname-CV.pdf — professional and searchable

## Reverse Chronological: The Default UK Format

The reverse chronological format — most recent job first — is the UK standard for 95% of applications. Recruiters expect it. ATS systems parse it reliably. Hiring managers scan it quickly.

Structure each entry:

**Job Title — Company Name, City**
**Month Year – Month Year**

- Achievement bullet
- Achievement bullet
- Achievement bullet

When reverse chronological works best:

- Steady career progression in one field
- No long gaps without explanation
- Recent roles are your strongest evidence

## Functional and Combination Formats: When to Use Them

**Functional CV** — Groups experience by skill rather than timeline. Rare in the UK and often viewed with suspicion by recruiters who want to see career progression clearly.

**Combination CV** — Opens with a strong skills summary, then reverse chronological experience. Useful for:

- Career changers highlighting transferable skills
- Candidates returning after a long break
- Specialists with diverse contract work

If you are changing careers, lead with a compelling personal statement and a "Key Skills" section, then show chronological history. Our [career change CV guide](/blog/career-change-cv-guide-uk) and [career change CV template](/templates/career-change-cv-uk) demonstrate this approach.

Avoid purely functional formats unless a careers adviser specifically recommends one for your situation.

## Formatting for Different Application Methods

**Online portals (NHS Jobs, Civil Service, company careers sites)** — ATS-safe PDF with standard headings. Test by copying text into plain text editor.

**Email direct to recruiter** — PDF attached with clear subject line: "Application for [Job Title] — [Your Name]". Same ATS-safe formatting applies.

**Recruitment agencies** — Word format sometimes preferred so consultants can reformat. Keep a .docx version with simple formatting.

**LinkedIn Easy Apply** — Profile must match your CV. Inconsistencies between LinkedIn dates and CV dates raise red flags.

**Speculative applications** — Shorter CV (one page) with a tailored personal statement explaining why you are contacting that company.

## ATS Considerations for UK Applications

Many UK employers — including NHS, councils, and large corporates — use ATS. Even if you're emailing a CV directly, online portals often parse uploads automatically.

Use [ATS-friendly templates](/templates) and follow our [ATS resume tips](/blog/top-ats-resume-tips) for the best results.

The format rules that protect ATS compatibility:

- Standard section headings
- No tables, text boxes, or columns with merged cells
- Contact info in the document body, not header/footer
- Simple bullet points and standard fonts
- Digitally created PDF, not a scanned image

## Common UK Format Mistakes

**Including a photo** — UK equality law discourages photos on CVs. They add no value and can introduce unconscious bias. Omit unless applying internationally where photos are expected.

**Listing full address** — City and region are sufficient. Full postcodes are unnecessary and raise privacy concerns.

**American spelling and date formats** — "Organized" and "03/15/2024" signal a non-UK application. Use British English and Jan 2024 – Mar 2024.

**Excessive design** — Infographic CVs fail on ATS and frustrate recruiters who want scannable text. Save creative layouts for portfolio roles.

**Including references with contact details** — "References available on request" is the UK norm. Listing referees upfront wastes space and exposes contacts to spam.

## Format by Career Stage

**School leaver / first job** — One page. Education first if no work history. Include part-time jobs, volunteering, and transferable skills. [Student CV template](/templates/student-cv-no-experience).

**Graduate** — One page, two at most with strong placement history. Lead with degree and relevant projects. [Graduate CV guide](/blog/graduate-cv-guide-uk).

**Mid-career (5–15 years)** — Two pages. Reverse chronological. Trim early roles to brief mentions. Focus on last 10 years in detail.

**Senior / executive** — Two pages maximum for most UK roles. Lead with strategic achievements, board-level impact, and P&L responsibility. [Executive premium resume template](/templates/executive-premium-resume-template).

**Returners and career changers** — Combination approach: strong personal statement, skills section, then chronological history. Address gaps honestly in your cover letter.
